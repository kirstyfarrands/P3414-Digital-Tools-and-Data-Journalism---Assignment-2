More Than a Performance: The Importance of Bad Bunny’s Super Bowl for Latino Communities

How Bad Bunny’s Super Bowl performance spoke to love, identity and the immigrant communities living under the shadow of ICE.

By Your Name

Bad Bunny’s Super Bowl halftime performance was more than a pop culture moment. It became a symbol of Latin identity on a global stage, while many of the people most represented by that visibility continue to live with fear shaped by immigration enforcement and public scrutiny.

In the months leading up to the performance, Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) had expanded deportation operations across the country. Arrests and raids increased, often affecting individuals with no criminal record, and reigniting fear across Latino communities. Families faced separation, and many began altering their daily routines, avoiding public spaces, work, or school in response. This made Bad Bunny's performance all the more meaningful and necessary for those Latino communities.

The Latin-inspired, Spanish-speaking performance became the 4th most viewed Super Bowl performance in history with 128.2 million live viewers in the US, while being the most watched global Super Bowl performance of all time at 4.157 billion views.

The 14-minute performance included a variety of Bad Bunny's most famous songs, with a vibrant, Puerto Rican-inspired set, ranging from sugar cane fields to Puerto Rican neighbourhoods in New York. The spectacle included cameos from artists such as Lady Gaga, as well as Puerto Rican singer Ricky Martin.

So what were symbolic moments from the performance?

Throughout the performance, a series of carefully constructed visual moments transformed the show into something more layered than a typical halftime spectacle. One of the most striking elements was the set design, which recreated scenes of Puerto Rican neighbourhood life—from small houses to street-style environments. Rather than using abstract staging, the performance grounded itself in everyday spaces, bringing a sense of familiarity and lived experience onto a global stage.

The performance recreated everyday Puerto Rican neighbourhood spaces on a global stage.

This visual approach reflects broader discussions around cultural representation in media, as explored by The Guardian, where identity is increasingly expressed through everyday lived environments rather than abstract symbolism.

The use of language also became a powerful symbolic choice. Performing primarily in Spanish, the artist resisted the expectation to conform to English-speaking audiences. Instead, the performance centred Spanish as the dominant language, subtly shifting the power dynamic. This positioned Spanish-language expression as something that does not require translation to achieve global impact, reflecting the growing influence of Latin music worldwide, highlighted by Billboard.

This moment is significant because it challenges the dominance of English within mainstream entertainment, reinforcing the legitimacy of Spanish-language identity on a global stage.

Costume and choreography further reinforced this sense of identity. Dancers and performers reflected styles associated with Puerto Rican and wider Latino culture, blending traditional influences with contemporary reggaeton aesthetics. The movement across the stage—at times celebratory, at times intimate—mirrored the rhythms of community life, rather than the polished uniformity often seen in large-scale performances.

Costume and choreography drew from Puerto Rican and Latino cultural influences.

By foregrounding culturally specific movement and style, the performance aligns with wider conversations about representation and identity in global media, discussed in research from Pew Research Center.

Camera work and staging also played a role in shaping meaning. Close-up shots of performers, crowd interactions, and moments of stillness created a sense of connection between the stage and the audience. These choices made the performance feel less like a distant spectacle and more like a shared cultural experience, inviting viewers into the space rather than simply observing it.

This sense of intimacy contrasts with the scale of the event, reinforcing how cultural storytelling can remain personal even within mass media environments, a theme explored in media analysis by The New York Times Arts section.

Together, these symbolic moments reshaped the performance into more than entertainment. The house-like set, the Spanish-language lyrics, the culturally rooted choreography, and the intimate staging all worked collectively to foreground identity and belonging. In doing so, the performance became a statement of visibility—asserting the presence of Latino culture within a space where it has not always been fully recognised.

Ultimately, the symbolism lies in how these elements work together, transforming the performance into a broader reflection of identity, resilience, and cultural visibility in contemporary society.

Who is Bad Bunny?

Bad Bunny, born Benito Antonio Martínez Ocasio, is a Puerto Rican global music star known for blending reggaeton and Latin trap into mainstream pop culture. He has become one of the most influential artists in the world, celebrated for his Spanish-language hits.

Benito recently demonstrated his commitment to the Latino community at the Grammys in February this year, a week before the Super Bowl performance. After making history to be the first Spanish language artist to win album of the year, Debí TiRAR MáS FOToS, he dedicated his win to immigrants, while expressing his disapproval to ICE.

Benito isn't a stranger to speaking out against US immigration. He recently expressed his concerns and issues with ICE and the US Government in an interview with i-D Magazine. In the interview, he discussed his decision not to tour in the USA for his 2025/26 world tour. Benito explained, "ICE could be outside [my concert]. And it’s something that we were talking about and very concerned about."

The Reality for the Latino Community

Bad Bunny’s Super Bowl performance didn’t directly mention ICE or deportation. Still, it reflected those realities in a more symbolic, cultural way that was closely tied to what Latino communities were experiencing at the time.

The reality for immigrant families in the USA has become a terrifying and fearful one with the increased presence of ICE raids across the country. A sharp rise in ICE arrests has occurred since Trump's second term in 2024, with the Latino immigrant community being a specific target.

Over half of Latinos (52%) are fearful of the threat of deportation, according to PEW research, while 62% of these immigrants are now worried they or someone they know will be deported from the US.

The Fear Facing Latino Communities

The threat of deportation for Latino communities in the USA has become so prominent that Bad Bunny refused to tour in the USA with fear that fans attending would be targeted by ICE.

The artist has chosen to tour elsewhere across the globe, including many Latin countries as well as European and Asian locations, away from ICE raids.
